* Welcome to Lesson 13!

You've learned all the individual features of ~emacs-writing~. Now it's time to tie everything together into a cohesive daily workflow that maximizes your productivity and creativity.

A good workflow is like a well-worn path through the forest - it gets you where you need to go efficiently, letting you focus on the journey (your writing) rather than navigation (the tools). This lesson shows you how to establish that path.

** Philosophy: Your Workflow is Personal

The workflow presented here is a *recommended starting point*, not a rigid prescription. Every writer is different:

- Some write in the morning, others at night
- Some plan extensively, others discover while writing
- Some write in long sessions, others in short bursts
- Some need silence, others need music

Use this lesson to find *your* optimal workflow, not to force yourself into someone else's pattern.

** The Core Principle: Minimize Friction

A good workflow removes obstacles between you and your writing:
- âœ“ Reduces decision fatigue (what do I do next?)
- âœ“ Automates repetitive tasks
- âœ“ Maintains focus and flow
- âœ“ Provides regular feedback (progress tracking)
- âœ“ Feels natural, not forced

If part of this workflow feels awkward, skip it! The best workflow is the one you actually use.

* The Three-Phase Daily Workflow

Your writing day has three distinct phases:

** Phase 1: Morning (Setup) - 5 minutes

*Goal*: Prepare your environment and mindset for writing.

*Activities*:
- Open your project
- Review yesterday's progress
- Set today's goal
- Activate writing mode

** Phase 2: Active Writing - 30-120 minutes

*Goal*: Write with minimal interruptions.

*Activities*:
- Write scenes
- Quick captures (characters, locations, notes)
- Occasional lookups (dictionary, synonyms)
- Stay in flow

** Phase 3: Evening (Wrap-up) - 10 minutes

*Goal*: Track progress, plan tomorrow, feel accomplished.

*Activities*:
- Count words
- Update tracking tables
- Review what you wrote
- Plan next session
- Backup/commit

Let's explore each phase in detail.

* Phase 2: Active Writing (30-120 Minutes)

This is where the magic happens. Your goal: stay in flow and minimize interruptions.

** The Golden Rule: Don't Break Flow

During active writing:
- âœ“ Write continuously
- âœ“ Accept imperfect first drafts
- âœ— Don't edit what you wrote yesterday
- âœ— Don't research extensively
- âœ— Don't check email/social media

Think of this as "creative mode" - editing comes later.

** Core Writing Loop

Your main activity is simple:

1. *Write narrative text*
   - Focus on the current scene
   - Let the words flow
   - Don't self-edit (yet)
   - Trust your revision process

2. *Finish scene*
   - Reach a natural stopping point
   - Leave a note if you know what comes next

3. *Insert next scene* (~F8 F8~ then ~s~)
   - Use the scene template
   - Fill in quick properties (PoV, Location)
   - Start writing immediately

4. *Repeat*

** Strategic Lookups: When to Use Language Tools

During active writing, minimize lookups. But sometimes you need the right word *now*.

*** When to Look Up Words

*DO look up* during writing:
- The exact word is on the tip of your tongue
- You're choosing between two similar words
- A wrong word will confuse the reader later

*DON'T look up* during writing:
- Minor word choice improvements (fix in revision)
- Extensive research (mark with [CHECK] and continue)
- Perfect synonyms (get close enough, polish later)

*** Quick Lookup Pattern

When you must look up a word:

1. Type the approximate word: "She felt melancholic"
2. Press ~F8 F8~ then ~d~ (dictionary)
3. Read definition quickly: "Yes, that's right"
4. Close lookup buffer: ~q~
5. Continue writing

*Time: 15 seconds*

If you're not sure: "She felt melancholic [CHECK - or use different word?]" and keep going.

** Managing TODO Markers

As you write, leave breadcrumbs for revision:

#+BEGIN_EXAMPLE
Alex walked into the coffee shop. [CHECK: Is it raining? Continuity.]

She saw Sam sitting in the corner booth. [TODO: Describe Sam's expression -
nervous? eager? What emotion sets up the conflict?]

"I found something," Sam said, sliding the envelope across the table.
[RESEARCH: What do old letters look like when they've been stored for 20 years?]
#+END_EXAMPLE

Markers to use:
- `[CHECK]` - Verify continuity or facts
- `[TODO]` - Something to add/expand
- `[RESEARCH]` - Need factual information
- `[REVISE]` - This section needs rewriting

These are gifts to your future editing self!

** The Pomodoro Integration

Many writers use the Pomodoro Technique (25 minutes writing, 5 minutes break):

*Writing block (25 min)*:
1. Start timer
2. Write continuously
3. Do quick captures only
4. When timer rings, finish your sentence

*Break (5 min)*:
- Stand up, stretch
- Refill water/coffee
- Don't check social media (breaks flow)
- Think about next scene

*After 4 pomodoros*: Take a longer break (15-30 min)

*Emacs Pomodoro*: Install ~org-pomodoro~ package for built-in timer

** Dealing with Stuck Moments

You're mid-scene and don't know what happens next:

*Option 1 - Skip Ahead*:
#+BEGIN_EXAMPLE
[SCENE INCOMPLETE - Pick up after the confrontation]

*** Next Scene - The Aftermath
#+END_EXAMPLE

Write what you *do* know, then return to the gap.

*Option 2 - Write Terribly*:
Give yourself permission to write badly. You can fix it later:

#+BEGIN_EXAMPLE
Sam tells Alex about the thing. [This is terrible dialogue, fix later]
Alex reacts with surprise. [Describe this better in revision]
#+END_EXAMPLE

Bad writing is better than no writing. Revision fixes everything.

*Option 3 - Quick Outline*:
#+BEGIN_EXAMPLE
# Outline for rest of scene:
# - Sam reveals the secret about the letters
# - Alex doesn't believe her at first
# - Sam shows proof
# - Alex realizes her whole understanding was wrong
# - Scene ends with Alex leaving abruptly

[Write the actual scene later]
#+END_EXAMPLE

Then move to the next scene you're excited about.

* Workflow Variations for Different Writers

The standard workflow might not fit everyone. Here are variations:

** The Pantser (Discovery Writer)

*Minimal planning, maximum spontaneity*

**Morning (3 min)**:
- Open manuscript
- Read last paragraph
- Activate writing mode
- Start writing (no goal setting, just write)

**Active Writing**:
- Write whatever comes
- Capture characters/locations as they appear
- Don't plan ahead
- Follow the story where it leads

**Evening (5 min)**:
- Count words
- Quick save/backup
- That's it!

**Weekly**: Brief check-in, less formal planning
 
** The Plotter (Detailed Planner)

*Extensive planning, structured execution*

**Morning (10 min)**:
- Review outline for today's scene
- Check character notes
- Review timeline
- Set specific scene goals
- Activate writing mode

**Active Writing**:
- Follow outline closely
- Frequent captures for new details
- Check character sheets often
- Maintain timeline consistency

**Evening (15 min)**:
- Update all tracking tables
- Review against outline
- Adjust plan if needed
- Update revision notes

**Weekly**: Detailed review, adjust outline, plan next chapters

** The Sprinter (Fast Drafter)

*High volume, fast pace*

**Morning (2 min)**:
- Open manuscript
- Activate writing mode
- Start timer
- GO!

**Active Writing**:
- Write as fast as possible
- Minimal captures (just mark [TODO])
- No lookups (just keep typing)
- Don't reread anything
- Multiple pomodoros back-to-back

**Evening (5 min)**:
- Count words (big numbers!)
- Save
- Done

**Weekly**: Big picture review, but don't slow down

** The Polisher (Careful Crafter)

*Slow, deliberate, high quality first draft*

**Morning (10 min)**:
- Reread yesterday's work
- Make minor edits
- Set today's goal (may be just 200-300 words)
- Activate focus mode

**Active Writing**:
- Write slowly and carefully
- Frequent dictionary lookups
- Crafted sentences
- Multiple revision passes on each paragraph
- Low word count, high quality

**Evening (15 min)**:
- Multiple reads of today's work
- Polish and edit
- Update tracking
- Detailed notes

**Weekly**: Less about quantity, more about quality review

** The Part-Timer (Limited Time)

*Only 30 minutes per day available*

**Morning (0 min)**:
- No morning routine
- Jump straight in

**Active Writing (25 min)**:
- Every second counts
- Minimal captures
- Write in sentence fragments if needed
- Stop mid-sentence if you must

**Evening (5 min)**:
- Quick count
- One-line note for tomorrow
- Save

**Weekly (1 hour)**:
- Do all the tracking/planning you skipped daily
- Review and organize notes

** The Binge Writer (Weekend Warrior)

*Writes in long sessions, few days per week*

**Session Start (10 min)**:
- Full review of story so far
- Check all timelines
- Set ambitious session goal (2000+ words)
- Prepare snacks and water

**Active Writing (3-4 hours)**:
- Multiple scenes in one session
- Take breaks every hour
- Quick captures
- Momentum is key

**Session End (20 min)**:
- Count everything
- Update all tables
- Extensive notes for next session
- Save and backup

**Weekly**: Not applicable (sessions ARE the weekly review)

* Common Workflow Challenges

** Challenge 1: "I can't maintain consistency"

*Solutions*:
- Lower your daily goal (100 words is fine!)
- Write at the same time every day (builds habit)
- Track your streak (don't break the chain)
- Remove friction (one-click project opening)
- Forgive missed days (restart tomorrow)

** Challenge 2: "I spend too much time planning/organizing"

*Solutions*:
- Set a timer for planning (max 5 minutes)
- Skip morning review sometimes
- Do captures AFTER writing session
- Remember: Organized notes don't write the book

** Challenge 3: "I keep editing yesterday's work"

*Solutions*:
- Use ~F8 F8 f~ (focus mode) - only see current scene
- Jump to end immediately (~M->~)
- Set a rule: No scrolling up!
- Start each session with new scene
- Save editing for designated revision time

** Challenge 4: "I get distracted by research"

*Solutions*:
- Mark [RESEARCH] and keep writing
- Set research time separate from writing time
- Use placeholder text: "She used a [TOOL NAME]"
- Remember: Fiction doesn't need perfect accuracy

** Challenge 5: "Progress tracking feels demotivating"

*Solutions*:
- Track words written (not % of goal)
- Celebrate small wins (any writing is good!)
- Use time-based goals instead of word count
- Skip tracking if it hurts more than helps
- Remember: Every writer's pace is different

** Challenge 6: "The workflow feels too rigid"

*Solutions*:
- Pick only the steps that work for you
- Modify the workflow to fit your style
- Some days, skip the whole workflow - just write!
- Workflows serve you, not the other way around

** Best Practices

1. *Consistency Over Intensity*: 30 minutes every day beats 5 hours once a week
2. *Minimize Friction*: Make starting effortless (aliases, bookmarks, routines)
3. *Separate Creation from Editing*: Write now, fix later
4. *Track Progress*: What gets measured gets improved
5. *Celebrate Wins*: Acknowledge every writing session
6. *Customize Freely*: Your workflow should feel natural
7. *Forgive Yourself*: Missed days happen - just restart
