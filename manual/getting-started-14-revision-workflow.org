* Welcome to Lesson 14!

You've finished your first draft - congratulations! Now comes the critical work of revision. This is where good writing becomes great writing. Fortunately, ~org-scribe~ provides powerful tools specifically designed for the revision process.

Revision isn't just fixing typos. It's about:
- Finding and fixing inconsistencies
- Strengthening character development
- Ensuring plot threads resolve properly
- Improving pacing and structure
- Polishing prose

This lesson shows you how to use ~org-scribe~'s features to make revision systematic, thorough, and even enjoyable.

By the end of this lesson, you'll have:
- Mastered editing mode for professional revision environment
- Learned to find inconsistencies using search functions
- Used timelines to check character development and plot balance
- Created and managed revision TODO lists systematically
- Developed a complete revision workflow
- Understood multi-pass revision strategies

*Estimated time*: 20-25 minutes

** The Revision Mindset

Revision requires a different mindset than drafting:

*Drafting mindset*:
- Creative, expansive
- "Yes, and..."
- Don't stop, don't edit
- Generate material

*Revision mindset*:
- Critical, selective
- "Is this necessary?"
- Slow down, examine closely
- Refine material

*Important*: These are different tasks requiring different mental states. Don't try to do both simultaneously!

** Types of Revision

This lesson covers three revision levels:

1. **Structural Revision** (Act/Chapter level) - Plot, pacing, character arcs
2. **Scene Revision** (Scene level) - Individual scenes, dialogue, description
3. **Line Editing** (Sentence level) - Prose polish, word choice, rhythm

We'll focus on how ~org-scribe~ supports each level.

* Revision Pass 2: Scene-Level Work

Now drill down to individual scenes. This is where editing mode shines!

** Using Editing Mode for Scene Revision

Your workflow:

1. *Left panel*: Click on a scene heading
2. *Center panel*: Scene appears for editing
3. *Right panel*: Read your notes for this scene
4. *Edit in center*: Make improvements
5. *Right panel*: Check off completed tasks
6. *Repeat* for next scene

** Scene Revision Checklist Template

Add this to your notes file for each scene:

#+BEGIN_EXAMPLE
*** Scene 1 - Opening

**** Opening Hook
- [ ] First sentence grabs attention?
- [ ] Clear genre/tone established?
- [ ] Inciting incident clear?

**** Character
- [ ] PoV character's voice distinct?
- [ ] Motivation clear?
- [ ] Emotional state shown (not told)?

**** Setting
- [ ] Location established quickly?
- [ ] Sensory details present (3+ senses)?
- [ ] Time of day clear?

**** Plot
- [ ] Scene advances plot?
- [ ] Conflict present?
- [ ] Stakes clear?
- [ ] Scene ends with forward momentum?

**** Dialogue
- [ ] Each character sounds different?
- [ ] Dialogue tags necessary? (could cut?)
- [ ] Subtext present?
- [ ] Natural rhythm?

**** Prose
- [ ] Strong verbs (not weak "was/were")?
- [ ] Active voice (not passive)?
- [ ] Unnecessary words cut?
- [ ] Sentence variety?

**** Technical
- [ ] POV consistent (no head-hopping)?
- [ ] Tense consistent?
- [ ] Timeline consistent with previous scenes?
#+END_EXAMPLE

** Scene Opening Improvements

Search for weak scene openings:

1. In center panel, position on a scene heading
2. Read first paragraph
3. Ask: Does this hook the reader?

*Weak opening example*:

#+BEGIN_EXAMPLE
*** Scene 5 - The Discovery

Alex woke up. She got out of bed. She went to the bathroom and brushed
her teeth. Then she went downstairs.
#+END_EXAMPLE

*Problems*:
- Boring routine activities
- Weak verbs ("woke up", "went")
- No conflict or tension
- Nothing at stake

*Improved opening*:

#+BEGIN_EXAMPLE
*** Scene 5 - The Discovery

The scream woke Alex at 3 AM. She bolted upright, heart hammering. Not
again. Not another nightmare about the accident.

Except‚Äîthe scream hadn't been in her dream.

It came from downstairs.
#+END_EXAMPLE

*Better*:
- Immediate tension (scream)
- Character emotion (fear)
- Mystery (what's downstairs?)
- Forward momentum (reader must continue)

** Strengthening Dialogue

Use dictionary tools for dialogue improvement:

1. Position cursor on dialogue
2. ~F8 F8 d~ (dictionary) - Check word meanings
3. ~F8 F8 y~ (synonyms) - Find better dialogue tags

*Example revision*:

Before:
#+BEGIN_EXAMPLE
"I don't believe you," Alex said.
"It's true," Sam said.
"Prove it," Alex said.
#+END_EXAMPLE

After using synonym lookup:
#+BEGIN_EXAMPLE
"I don't believe you," Alex snapped.
"It's true." Sam's voice was barely a whisper.
Alex leaned forward. "Prove it."
#+END_EXAMPLE

*Improvements*:
- Varied dialogue tags
- Body language added
- Action breaks up dialogue
- More dynamic exchange

** Cutting Unnecessary Scenes

Sometimes the best revision is deletion!

*Scene to cut if*:
- Doesn't advance plot
- Doesn't develop character
- Doesn't reveal information
- Could be summarized in one sentence
- Reader won't miss it

*How to check*:

Ask: "If I delete this scene, what's lost?"

If answer is "Nothing important" ‚Üí Cut it!

*Process*:
1. In outline (left panel), identify suspect scene
2. In center, read scene critically
3. In notes (right), record decision:

#+BEGIN_EXAMPLE
** Scene 4 - Lunch at Caf√©
- Purpose: Alex and Sam eat lunch
- Advances plot? No
- Character development? Minimal
- New information? No
- Decision: CUT
- Replacement: Brief mention in Scene 5 ("After lunch...")
- Status: [ ] Not cut yet
#+END_EXAMPLE

Mark scene with ~ARCHIVE~ tag to hide it without deleting:

#+BEGIN_EXAMPLE
*** Scene 4 - Lunch at Caf√©    :ARCHIVE:
#+END_EXAMPLE

Then ~C-c C-x a~ (org-archive-subtree) moves it to archive file.

* Revision Pass 3: Line Editing

The final pass polishes prose at sentence level.

** Line-by-Line Reading

Read slowly, out loud if possible:

1. *Left panel*: Select scene
2. *Center panel*: Read every sentence
3. Mark problems inline:

#+BEGIN_EXAMPLE
Alex walked [WEAK VERB: strode? hurried?] to the door and opened it.
Sam was standing [WEAK: stood?] there with a package in his hands
[REDUNDANT: "in his hands" implied]. He was [PASSIVE: "He looked"?]
nervous.
#+END_EXAMPLE

** Common Prose Problems to Fix

*** Weak Verbs

*Find*: Search for "was", "were", "is", "are"

#+BEGIN_EXAMPLE
C-s was RET
#+END_EXAMPLE

Many instances need stronger verbs:

Before: "She was angry"
After: "She seethed"

Before: "The door was open"
After: "The door hung ajar"

*** Passive Voice

*Find*: Search for "was [verb]ed" pattern

Before: "The letter was opened by Alex"
After: "Alex tore open the letter"

Before: "Dinner was being prepared"
After: "The cook prepared dinner"

*Note*: Sometimes passive voice is correct! Don't automatically change all instances.

*** Filter Words

These create distance between reader and character:

- saw
- heard
- felt
- thought
- realized
- wondered
- seemed
- appeared

*Example*:

Before: "Alex heard the door slam"
After: "The door slammed"

Before: "She felt her heart racing"
After: "Her heart raced"

We're in Alex's PoV - we know she hears/feels these things!

*** Repetition

Use search to find repeated words:

#+BEGIN_EXAMPLE
C-s suddenly RET
(press C-s repeatedly to see all instances)
#+END_EXAMPLE

If "suddenly" appears 15 times in one chapter - cut most of them!

*Tracking in notes*:

#+BEGIN_EXAMPLE
* Overused Words

** Chapter 1
- "suddenly": 8 times (cut to 2-3)
- "just": 12 times (cut to 3-4)
- "really": 6 times (cut all)

** Chapter 2
- "very": 9 times (replace with stronger adjectives)
- "that": 23 times (try cutting, many unnecessary)
#+END_EXAMPLE

** Using Language Tools in Revision

During line editing, use dictionary tools heavily:

*Finding better words*:

1. Spot weak word: "The house was big"
2. ~F8 F8 y~ (synonyms)
3. Search: "big"
4. Find alternatives: enormous, vast, sprawling, cavernous
5. Pick best fit: "The house sprawled across the lot"

*Checking meanings*:

1. Unsure about word choice: "melancholic"
2. ~F8 F8 d~ (dictionary)
3. Read definition
4. Confirm it's the right emotional tone
5. Keep or change based on nuance

** Rhythm and Pacing

Read sentences aloud:

*Check for*:
- Sentence variety (mix short and long)
- Paragraph length (vary)
- Repetitive structures

*Before (monotonous)*:

#+BEGIN_EXAMPLE
Alex walked to the door. She opened it slowly. She saw Sam standing
there. He looked nervous. She asked him what he wanted.
#+END_EXAMPLE

All sentences same length and structure!

*After (varied)*:

#+BEGIN_EXAMPLE
Alex approached the door and eased it open. Sam stood there, fidgeting.

"What do you want?" she asked.
#+END_EXAMPLE

Varied sentence structure, better rhythm.

** Final Consistency Check

* Complete Revision Workflow

Here's a systematic approach integrating all tools:

** Week 1: Structural Pass

*Monday*:
1. Open manuscript
2. ~F8 F8 e~ (activate editing mode)
3. Generate plot timeline (~C-c C-c~)
4. Record issues in notes panel
5. Work time: 2 hours
6. Exit mode (~F8 F8 e~)

*Tuesday*:
1. ~F8 F8 e~
2. Generate character timeline (~C-c C-c~)
3. Record issues in notes panel
4. Work time: 2 hours

*Wednesday*:
1. ~F8 F8 e~
2. Review chapter structure (outline panel)
3. Identify scenes to cut/add
4. Record in notes panel
5. Work time: 2 hours

*Thursday*:
1. Review all structural notes
2. Prioritize issues (high/medium/low)
3. Create action plan
4. Work time: 1 hour

*Friday*:
1. ~F8 F8 e~
2. Begin fixing structural issues
3. Add missing scenes
4. Merge/split chapters as needed
5. Work time: 3 hours

** Week 2-3: Scene-Level Pass

*Daily routine (Mon-Fri)*:

Morning session (1-2 hours):
1. ~F8 F8 e~
2. Select next DRAFT scene (outline panel)
3. Read scene against checklist (notes panel)
4. Identify problems
5. Don't fix yet - just mark issues

Afternoon session (1-2 hours):
1. Return to scenes marked in morning
2. Make edits
3. Strengthen openings
4. Improve dialogue
5. Add sensory details
6. Mark scene as REVISED

Target: 2-3 scenes per day

** Week 4: Line Editing Pass

*Daily routine (Mon-Fri)*:

Full day session (4-6 hours):
1. ~F8 F8 e~
2. Read scene aloud
3. Fix weak verbs
4. Eliminate passive voice
5. Cut filter words
6. Improve rhythm
7. Use ~F8 F8 y~ for synonym lookup
8. Use ~F8 F8 d~ for word verification
9. Mark scene as POLISHED

Target: 3-5 scenes per day

** Week 5: Final Check

*Monday-Tuesday*: Full manuscript read-through
- Read as a reader (not writer)
- Mark remaining issues
- Don't stop to fix (maintain flow)

*Wednesday-Thursday*: Fix issues from read-through
- Work through marked problems
- Final consistency checks
- Update tracking tables

*Friday*: Completion
- Verify all TODO items cleared
- Generate final word count
- Update progress to 100%
- Celebrate!

* Multi-Pass Strategy Examples

Different writers use different revision approaches:

** The Three-Pass Method

*Pass 1 - Macro (1-2 weeks)*:
- Plot structure
- Character arcs
- Pacing
- Major additions/cuts

*Pass 2 - Micro (2-3 weeks)*:
- Scene by scene
- Dialogue
- Description
- Individual paragraphs

*Pass 3 - Polish (1 week)*:
- Sentence level
- Word choice
- Rhythm
- Final cleanup

** The Five-Pass Method

*Pass 1 - Plot*: Story structure only
*Pass 2 - Character*: Character development only
*Pass 3 - Scene*: Scene-level improvements
*Pass 4 - Prose*: Line editing
*Pass 5 - Consistency*: Final cleanup

Each pass = 1 week

** The Chapter-at-a-Time Method

For each chapter:
1. Structural review (1 day)
2. Scene improvements (2-3 days)
3. Line editing (1-2 days)
4. Final check (1 day)

Complete one chapter fully before moving to next.

*Advantage*: Each chapter is fully finished as you go
*Disadvantage*: Harder to maintain overall consistency

** Choose Your Approach

*Fast drafters*: Multi-pass (more rounds, faster)
*Slow crafters*: Chapter-at-a-time (fewer rounds, thorough)
*Plotters*: Separate passes for plot/character/prose
*Pantsers*: All-in-one intensive revision

*Experiment to find what works for you!*

** Best Practices

1. *Separate creation from revision* - Don't revise while drafting
2. *Use editing mode consistently* - Professional environment matters
3. *Work systematically* - Don't jump around randomly
4. *Record before fixing* - Identify all issues before solving any

6. *Read aloud* - Ear catches what eye misses
7. *Track progress* - Stay motivated with visible completion
8. *Trust the process* - Revision makes good writing great
